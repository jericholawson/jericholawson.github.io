CautionEthical Considerations

For several years, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency published a map called EJScreen. The map included layers for socioeconomic indicators like race, income, and age, along with layers for environmental indicators such as TRI releases, proximity to superfund sites, proximity to major traffic sources, and more. Users could also map layers for health disparities, such as cancer rates and asthma rates. It helped document how certain communities faced disproportionate environmental burdens across the country. On February 5, 2025, EJScreen was removed from the EPA’s website, at the same time that the EPA was overhauling several diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives under the Trump administration. Luckily, the advocacy organization Public Environmental Data Partners (PEDP) archived a reconstructed version of the map and made it available here. This one example of several government data sources that have been suppressed for political reasons. Why do you think these data removals are happening? What are some of the consequences of removing or defunding public government data like this? What role should non-governmental organizations play in securing access to this data? Are there ever good political reasons to suppress public data, and who should be involved those decisions? Share your ideas on our discussions Slack channel.
