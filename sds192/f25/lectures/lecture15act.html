Behind the AI

Despite over-analyzing the six-worded sentence, it is important to understand the mechanics of this sentence generation when it comes to generating responses. We will need to assign probabilities (and/or weights) to how the next word of a sentence comes about.

There are many sophisticated models that can be used for natural language processing, including but not limited to:

  • Deep learning models
  • Neural networks
  • Large-language models

We will not delve into these specific models; rather, we will focus on a more simplistic model to garner a better understanding of how a probabilistic model can fuel generation of a sentence.

I have created two models to aid in our creation of sentences:

  • A model to generate probabilities of certain words being used, given their part of speech (i.e. noun, adjective)
  • A model to generate probabilities of certain parts of speech being used next given the part of speech from the previous word

These two models allow for the determination of a word given what part of speech we’re using and what the next word might be given what word we’ve used. As you’ll see in a second, this approach is rather interesting.

To incorporate these two models and generate sentences, we will use two different datasets:

  • wordData.csv: This contains 114 different words with the part of speech they fall under, their usage probability, and the chance of which part of speech comes next.
    • Words come from the list of most commonly used words in English (Source: Wikipedia)
  • tranMatData.csv: This is a transition matrix that allows us to go from one word to the next.

A transition matrix can be thought of as a grid of outcomes/chances depending on the action. A small example is to determine what mood you’ll be in the next day given how you feel currently. If you’re in a bad mood today, perhaps there’s a 60% chance you’ll be in a good mood tomorrow. On the flip side, if you’re in a good mood today, perhaps there will be a 45% chance you’ll be in a good mood tomorrow. When placed in a transition matrix, we get the following:

Happy Sad
Happy 0.45 0.55
Sad 0.60 0.40

Running the Model

Now onto the fun part – we are going to create our own sentences! To do so, run the following chunk below:

# Code to provide the first word of the sentence
sentence = c("the")
wordInd = which(wordData$word == "the")

# We can specify the number of words we want in our sentence here
numWords = 6

# Run this code to create the sentence
for (i in 1:(numWords - 1)){
  wordInd = sample(1:nrow(wordData), size = 1, prob = tranMatData[wordInd, ])
  sentence = c(sentence, wordData$word[wordInd])
}

# Print the sentence here
paste(sentence, collapse = " ")
[1] "the person by man be eye"

There you have it – we’ve generated sentences! But as you see, the results are interesting to say the least. You’ll notice that every time you run it, you will generate a new sentence. Some may make sense, while others may not.

Wrapup

Thankfully, these sophisticated programs are able to answer questions and provide responses that answer our questions, do so in a natural way, and give additional information aside from the initial ask. This is what has made these programs so intriguing and highly used over the last few years.

However, as you’ve seen with this example, developing these models takes a lot of foresight and power. The factors you’ve discussed in this activity only scratch the surface of all potential variables and factors to consider when developing a model. Additionally, we only looked at prompt generation and not into other avenues, such as comprehending prompts.

If you’ve already exhausted a lot of brain power thinking about these factors, imagine the power it takes to factor these considerations into a series of models and executing the results. The data itself will also compound these actions. Also, as you’ve seen here, artificial intelligence relies on the success of the models, data, and processes behind the scenes. Nothing is perfect, and at times, you may find that these models will produce odd, incomplete, or unrelated answers.
